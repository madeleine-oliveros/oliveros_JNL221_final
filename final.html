The revision of the FBI definition of rape highlights consent and gender dynamics in the United States
By Madeleine Oliveros

Content Warning: This article contains discussion of sexual assault

Sexual assault is an issue that has plagued the United States for hundreds of years. In data from the United Nations Office of Drugs and Crime, for the years 2003 to 2008, the United States had the most individual counts of sexual assault in the world.

According to the data, the counts ranged from 89,000 to over 95,000 in those years. The data also mentioned in an additional comments section that the numbers shown for the United States collected by the FBI only counted offenses against women. According to the FBI Uniform Crime Reporting Program’s Rape Addendum, the definition of rape used by the FBI for 80 years was “carnal knowledge of a female forcibly and against her will.”

In 2013, the FBI put a new definition of rape into effect. According to the FBI Rape Addendum, the definition, beginning in 2013 is “Penetration, no matter how slight, of the vagina or anus with any body part or object, or oral penetration by a sex organ of another person, without the consent of the victim.”

The updated definition created a new nationwide standard for collecting more inclusive and accurate rape data. Since the definition was changed, counts of reported rape increased. A dataset from the FBI’s violent crimes page contains the counts of rape under the original definition along with counts under the revised definition from 2013 to 2019. According to numbers in the FBI data, the reported rate of rape in the United States rose by an average of about 38.9% when applying the new definition.
